# Product

## Register

product

## Users

ChatUFO is for the curious public: people who want to explore declassified UAP and UFO material without needing archival training, database skills, or prior knowledge of the document set. They may be casual readers, skeptical investigators, journalists, students, or hobby researchers, but their common need is the same: ask a question, find the source, and read the original page for themselves.

Users arrive with mixed trust levels. Some are curious, some are skeptical, and some are already deep in the topic. The interface should meet all of them with clarity, restraint, and cited evidence rather than spectacle.

## Product Purpose

ChatUFO is a public research tool for querying a page-indexed archive of declassified UAP records, sightings, government documents, field reports, and related source material. Its job is to make the archive easier to search, browse, cite, and cross-reference while keeping the original documents visible and authoritative.

Success means a user can move from a natural-language question to a grounded answer, then immediately verify that answer against the exact cited page. The product should reduce friction around discovery and reading without making claims beyond what the corpus supports.

## Brand Personality

Credible and source-first.

The product should feel like a clean public reading room with a disciplined terminal/archive edge. The existing monochrome, cyan, grid, page-preview, and HUD language are part of the identity, but they should remain refined, legible, and useful. The tone is calm, skeptical, precise, and evidence-led.

## Anti-references

Do not make ChatUFO feel like a generic SaaS AI chat product. Avoid glossy assistant branding, padded marketing cards, soft gradient dashboards, oversized testimonial-style claims, and vague productivity language.

Do not make it feel like a conspiracy site. Avoid sensationalism, alien-fantasy imagery, fear cues, dramatic red-alert styling, paranormal theatrics, and copy that implies conclusions the archive does not prove.

Do not overplay the terminal aesthetic until it becomes a gimmick. The HUD language should support orientation, source inspection, and archive navigation, not compete with the documents.

## Design Principles

Source before synthesis. Every answer, preview, and summary should point users back to the original page as the final authority.

Curiosity without sensationalism. The product can feel intriguing, but the interface should stay calm and skeptical.

Archive as interface. Document pages, metadata, citations, page images, and search results are the product's core visual material.

Refine the existing language. Keep the terminal/archive identity, but make it cleaner, more readable, and less noisy where it affects comprehension.

Trust through restraint. Use precise labels, clear states, and modest motion. Avoid visual or copy choices that make the product feel like it is selling a conclusion.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA for contrast, focus visibility, keyboard navigation, and readable text states.

Long-form document reading should remain comfortable: line length, spacing, scrolling behavior, source previews, and citation interactions need to support extended use.

All important citation, document, filter, and chat states should be understandable without relying only on color.

Motion and atmospheric effects such as scanlines, CRT movement, grain, and carousel behavior should respect reduced-motion preferences and never interfere with reading, selection, or source verification.
